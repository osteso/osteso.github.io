When working as a researcher in theoretical cosmology, I devoted an important part of time to the study of oscillons. They are bubbles of high-energy that may form shortly after cosmic inflation and are sursprisingly stable. In some theoretical models, they can even live long enough to become the dominant form of energy of the universe. In most models, however, this is not the case. Studying the properties of oscillons, I found, together with my colleagues at the University of Basel, that they can produce copious amounts of gravitational waves. We published this result in Physical Review Letters, one of the scientific journals with the highest impact factor. This led to quite a bit of publicity for our research and a press-release on the universityâ€™s website. Shortly after, articles on our research started appearing on news websites and I was contacted by a journalist at INAF, the italian national institute for astrophysics. We exchanged some emails and he wrote this article (in italian).
Since then, we continued our research on oscillons, confirming our results and extending them to cosmological models derived from string theory. Plenty of questions remain on the impact of oscillons in the early universe but I would not be surprised if they actually existed and played an important role in the history of the universe. My experience as a researcher was that they were showing up in a very vast class of theoretical models.
